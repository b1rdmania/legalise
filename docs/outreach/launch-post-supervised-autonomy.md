# Launch Post - Supervised Autonomy / Audit Trail

Working launch copy. Keep this as raw material, not final public copy. The
strongest angle is supervised autonomy, with audit as the liability receipt.

## X / LinkedIn Draft

The interesting question is no longer only:

"What can AI automate?"

It is:

"What must remain supervised?"

That is the line Legalise is exploring.

Legalise is an open-source UK legal AI workspace for supervised autonomy.

Open a matter.
Ask the assistant.
Install legal modules.
Run them through human-approved gates.
Keep the record.

Why the audit trail?

Not because audit logs are exciting.

Because professional liability is boring until it is existential.

If AI touches a matter, the solicitor remains accountable.

The client may ask what happened.
The insurer may ask what happened.
The SRA may ask what happened.
A partner may ask why a deadline, citation, disclosure decision, or letter went wrong.

A solicitor cannot responsibly supervise something if they cannot reconstruct:

- what material the system saw
- whether privileged material was involved
- which model or module touched it
- what the system produced
- who approved or relied on it
- what changed after human review

Without that, "supervised autonomy" is just "trust me, a lawyer was nearby."

Legalise records the path: what the module saw, what it did, what it produced, what posture it ran under, and where human supervision sits.

The audit trail is not the product.

It is the receipt.

What this is not:

Legalise v0.4 is not claiming to solve every hard problem in legal AI.

It is not:

- a law firm
- legal advice
- for live client matters
- a guarantee against hallucination
- a case-law authority engine
- a substitute for solicitor judgment
- a promise that cloud models are suitable for every matter
- a complete regulator-grade supervision system

Evals, hallucination controls, cloud/local routing, prompt shrouding, and intricate case-law verification are deliberately not the v0.4 claim.

They matter.

They are engineering problems.

They are not magic.

In 2026, a competent small team can build serious harnesses, evals, grounding checks, local-model routes, refusal policies, and red-team loops in weeks, not years.

The harder question is not whether the model can be made less wrong.

The harder question is whether the legal workflow knows who is responsible when it is wrong.

That is what Legalise is exploring first.
